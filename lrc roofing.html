03

Insurance claim work

gets botched constantly — adjusters miss damage, contractors don't document properly, and homeowners get stuck eating the difference between the check and the actual cost.

04

Ventilation and decking

are the two things almost nobody inspects, and the two things that actually determine whether your new roof lasts 15 years or 35.

Why Most Siding Projects Go Sideways

New siding can hide problems for years — and then cost you everything.

The visible part of a siding install is the easy part. What determines whether your house stays dry for the next four decades is the flashing details, the house wrap overlap, and how the trim ties into the windows. Most contractors get this wrong because nobody's watching.

01

Old siding gets ripped off in a hurry

without checking the underlying sheathing for rot or water damage. New siding goes back over the same problem, sealed in for another decade.

02

House wrap gets installed sloppy or skipped entirely

— upside-down, lapped wrong, or with tears taped over instead of replaced. Tyvek only works if it's installed exactly the way the manufacturer specifies.

03

Window and door flashing details

are where 80% of long-term siding failures actually start. Most crews don't have a flashing standard. We do — and it's documented in your proposal.

04

Generic trim packages

make a custom home look like a tract subdivision. The trim profile around your windows, fascia, and corners is the single biggest curb-appeal lever in the project.

Why Most Custom Carpentry Projects Go Sideways

Production carpentry has gotten really fast. And really sloppy.

Most decks built today are framed with the same speed and care as a tract garage. The structure is fine — but the joinery, the proportions, the finish detail? That's the difference between something you're proud to host on, and something that looks fine until you really look at it.

01

Concrete pier foundations get rushed

or undersized for the deck's load and snow weight. The deck looks fine for years, then starts to sag at the corners.

02

Composite decking gets installed wrong

— gaps incorrectly spaced, fasteners showing, end-cuts left unsealed. Trex and TimberTech only perform if they're installed exactly the way the manufacturer specifies.

03

Pergola proportions look off

because the crew has built ten of them this year and just defaults to standard dimensions instead of scaling to your specific home and yard.

04

Hardware and lighting integration

gets thrown in as an afterthought. Cable rail systems, low-voltage step lighting, ceiling fan pre-wire — all of it should be planned in framing, not bolted on after.

and minimum code. 2x4 walls instead of 2x6, batt insulation only, builder-grade everything. The home looks great at closing and starts compromising 5 years in.

02

Owner-builder companies that don't actually build

use the term loosely — meaning the owner shows up at sales meetings and disappears once the contract is signed. The actual build is run by whichever super has the bandwidth.

03

Mechanical layout is usually an afterthought

— HVAC chases, plumbing runs, electrical home-runs all get squeezed into whatever space is left after framing. Done well, this is invisible. Done poorly, you can hear it for thirty years.

04

Punch list culture

is broken in production building. Move-in dates get pushed to make sales numbers, then the homeowner spends the first 12 months trying to get someone to come fix things.
